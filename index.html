Applying Derivatives to Malaysian Agriculture

As a Food Security student in Malaysia, understanding the exact moment crop production peaks or slows down is vital. This tool simulates how calculus optimizes the lifecycle of the **Elaeis guineensis** (African Oil Palm), the backbone of Malaysia's agricultural economy.

📋 The Bioscience Scenario

Oil palm trees have a productive lifespan of roughly 25 to 30 years. Yields follow a distinct physiological curve: slow growth initially, a steep surge in productivity, a peak plateau, and a gradual biological decline.

The Mission: We model the cumulative fresh fruit bunch (FFB) yield over time. By taking the first derivative $f'(x)$, we pinpoint the exact rate of production growth to help estate managers time their replanting schedules perfectly.

🧮 The Mathematical Model

Based on typical growth dynamics parameterized from MPOB (Malaysian Palm Oil Board) yield trends.

Cumulative Yield Function $f(x)$
$f(x) = \frac{180}{1 + e^{-0.3(x - 12)}}$
Where $x$ = Tree Age (Years), $f(x)$ = Total Cumulative Yield (Tonnes/Hectare)
First Derivative $f'(x)$ (Rate of Change)
$f'(x) = \frac{54 \cdot e^{-0.3(x - 12)}}{(1 + e^{-0.3(x - 12)})^2}$
Yielding: **Annual Production Velocity** (Tonnes/Hectare/Year)
